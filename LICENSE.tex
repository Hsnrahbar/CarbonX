\chapter{Introduction}
\label{ch:intro}

Carbon nanotubes (CNTs) exhibit exceptional electrical, optical, and mechanical properties, making them critical candidates for applications in energy storage, sensing, and advanced composites. While chemical vapor deposition (CVD) offers advantages in scalability and cost-effectiveness, controlling CNT morphology in floating-catalyst CVD (FCCVD) reactors remains challenging due to the complex interplay between chemical kinetics, catalyst nanoparticle evolution, and carbon deposition mechanisms. \texttt{CarbonX} is an \textbf{object-oriented Python package} designed to simulate FCCVD reactors and predict the gas-phase synthesis of single- and multi-walled CNTs alongside metallic (Fe, Ni, and Co) nanoparticles. The framework's modular architecture integrates four fully coupled submodules: a \textbf{chemical kinetics submodule} for gas-phase reactions; a \textbf{surface kinetics submodule} accounting for catalyst activation, deactivation, and hydrogenation; a \textbf{particle dynamics submodule} for tracking nanoparticle evolution through inception, surface growth, coagulation, and sintering; and a \textbf{CNT dynamics submodule} for predicting nanotube length, diameter, and wall number. A defining feature of \texttt{CarbonX} is its \textbf{extensible design}, which allows users to seamlessly integrate \textbf{new modules}—such as custom \textbf{surface kinetics} or \textbf{gas kinetics} mechanisms—into the current code without modifying the core simulation engine. This flexibility enables the adaptation of substrate-based models to the dynamic FCCVD environment. Furthermore, an integrated \textbf{machine learning submodule} classifies parametric maps to identify the specific process conditions (e.g., temperature, pressure, and catalyst concentration) required for targeted CNT formation. All submodules have been comprehensively benchmarked against experimental measurements of nanoparticle size distributions and CNT growth kinetics across multiple reactor configurations, providing a validated foundation for predictive modeling.

\section{Why Python?}
\label{sec:opensource}

\texttt{CarbonX} is an object-oriented Python package designed to simulate FCCVD reactors by integrating distinct, fully coupled submodules that handle chemical kinetics, surface kinetics, particle dynamics, and carbon nanotube (CNT) evolution. The choice of Python allows the framework to seamlessly incorporate powerful specialized packages like Cantera for detailed gas-phase analysis and machine learning tools for process optimization, while its extensible design enables users to easily add their own modules, such as custom surface or gas kinetics, without altering the core simulation engine. This modular approach, combined with Python’s capacity for continuous integration, ensures the code remains stable and reproducible as researchers adapt the software to model complex interactions between catalyst nanoparticles and carbon deposition across various reactor configurations. \chapter{What is currently included?}

\label{sec:specify}

\texttt{CarbonX} is designed as a process design simulation tool and does not aim to model the entirety of CNT/graphene physics produced in every specific reactor. As such, users are responsible for evaluating whether \texttt{CarbonX} is the appropriate tool for their specific application. The \textit{current} version of \texttt{CarbonX} includes a solver to model 1D plug-flow reactors for the production of metallic nanoparticles and carbon nanotubes. The \textit{current} version includes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Modules to simulate the following phenomena:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Agglomeration, surface growth, and sintering of metallic (e.g., Ni, Fe, and Co) nanoparticles.
        \item Inception of Fe from iron pentacarbonyl (Fe(CO)$_5$).
        \item Pyrolysis of hydrocarbon feedstock gases (e.g., C$_2$H$_2$, C$_2$H$_4$, CH$_4$, etc.) using:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Available chemical kinetics models (e.g., FFCM-2 \cite{ZDV2023}, Caltech \cite{blanquart2009chemical}, ABF \cite{appel2000kinetic}).
            \item User-defined detailed chemical kinetics mechanisms.
        \end{itemize}
        \item Surface kinetics modeling for:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item C$_2$H$_2$ surface kinetics on Fe nanoparticle surfaces, accounting for adsorption, dissociation, and desorption of C$_2$H$_2$, dehydrogenation, as well as nanoparticle carburization, saturation, and deactivation.
            \item Custom hydrocarbons on nanoparticle surfaces using user-defined surface kinetics mechanisms.
        \end{itemize}
        \item Formation of graphene layers on the surface of nanoparticles.
        \item Formation of single-walled (SWCNTs) and multi-walled (MWCNTs) carbon nanotubes on the surface of nanoparticles.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Post-processing module to obtain:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Elemental mass balance profiles.
        \item Size distribution profiles for nanoparticles and carbon nanotubes.
        \item Non-dimensionalized self-preserving size distribution profiles.
        \item 2D parametric maps of CNT characteristics, such as diameter, length, and number of walls.
        \item Other process parameters (e.g., density, viscosity, species concentration, and nanoparticle carbon impurity) provided as an output (.csv) file.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\section{Chemical kinetics}
\label{sec:Chemical_kinetics}
The chemical kinetics submodule in the \texttt{CarbonX} package uses Cantera \cite{cantera11}, an open-source suite for reacting flows and thermodynamic calculations.\ Cantera numerically solves the coupled set of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) governing the time- or space-dependent evolution of species mass fractions in chemically reacting systems. For a homogeneous gas mixture, the mass conservation equation can be written as:
\begin{equation}
    \label{cantera_mass_conserv}
    \frac{dY\subtxt{i}}{dt} = \frac{\dot{\omega}\subtxt{i}m\subtxt{i}}{\rho\subtxt{g}}
\end{equation}
in which $Y\subtxt{i}$ is the mass fraction, $\dot{\omega}\subtxt{i}$ is the net molar production rate per unit volume of gas, $\rho\subtxt{g}$ is the density of the carrier gas, and $m\subtxt{i}$ is the molecular mass of gas species $i$.

The term $\dot{\omega}\subtxt{i}$ is calculated based on the elementary reaction mechanism provided as a YAML input file. Each reaction $r$ contributes to the net production rate of gas species $i$ as:
\begin{equation}
    \label{cantera_source_term}
    \dot{\omega}\subtxt{i} = \sum_{r}{\nu\subtxt{i,r}(k\subtxt{frw,r}\prod_j[C\subtxt{j}]^{\nu^{\prime}\subtxt{j,r}} - k\subtxt{rev,r}\prod_j[C\subtxt{j}]^{\nu^{\prime\prime}\subtxt{j,r}})}
\end{equation}
in which $k\subtxt{frw,r}$ and $k\subtxt{rev,r}$ are forward and reverse rate coefficients, ${\nu^{\prime}\subtxt{j,r}}$ and ${\nu^{\prime\prime}\subtxt{j,r}}$ are the stoichiometric coefficients of reactants and products $j$, and $\nu\subtxt{i,r}$ is the stoichiometric coefficient of species $i$, all in the reaction $r$. While $k\subtxt{frw,r}$ is directly determined from the Arrhenius expression using rate constant parameters from the kinetic mechanism input file, $k\subtxt{rev,r}$ is calculated using $k\subtxt{frw,r}$ and equilibrium constant, $K\subtxt{eq,r}$, which is calculated using the thermodynamic properties given as NASA polynomial coefficients \cite{mcbride2002nasa}, defined for each species in the input mechanism file.

\section{Particle dynamics: population balance model}
\label{sec:Particle_Dynamics}
\ The \texttt{CarbonX} package employs particle dynamics models (both monodisperse and sectional population balance) \cite{tsantilis2002primary} to describe the evolution of the morphology of catalyst NPs during inception, surface growth, agglomeration, and sintering in FCCVD reactors, knowing their characteristics such as  their PP $d\subtxt{p}$ and mobility ($d\subtxt{m}$) diameters, and number of PP per agglomerate ($n\subtxt{p}$), which are determined by tracking agglomerate number density ($N$), volume concentration ($V$), and area concentration ($A$). 
The 1D (volume-based) sectional population balance model (SPBM) is mathematically described by the general dynamic equation as \cite{kumar1996solution}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:general_pbm}
\begin{split}
\frac{\partial n(v,t)}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \big(G(v) \, n(v,t)\big)}{\partial v} = {} & \frac{1}{2} \int_{0}^{\infty} n(v-v',t) \, n(v',t) \, \beta(v-v',v') \, dv' \\
& - n(v,t) \int_{0}^{\infty} n(v',t) \, \beta(v,v') \, dv' + S(v)
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $n(v,t)$ represents the density function of particles with volume between $v$ and $v + dv$ at time $t$, $G(v)$ is the size-dependent growth rate, $\beta(v, v')$ denotes the Brownian collision frequency between particles of sizes $v$ and $v'$, and $S(v)$ is the inception rate of particles of size $v$. Eq.~\ref{eq:general_pbm} can be discretized by dividing the particle-size range into sections (bins), where section $i$ is represented by a representative volume $x_i$ and by the particle number and area concentrations $N_i$ and $A_i$, respectively.

\subsection{Agglomeration}

During NP agglomeration, the rate of change of \( N \) in the section $i$ is calculated as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dNdt_sectional}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{dN\subtxt{i}}{dt}\bigg|\subtxt{agg} = \sum_{x\subtxt{i-1} \leq x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k} \leq x\subtxt{i}}^{i \geq j \geq k}\left(1-\frac{1}{2} \delta\subtxt{j,k}\right) \beta\subtxt{j,k} N\subtxt{j} N\subtxt{k}\left(\frac{x\subtxt{i-1}-x\subtxt{j} - x\subtxt{k}}{x\subtxt{i-1}-x\subtxt{i}}\right) \rho\subtxt{g} \\
+ \sum_{x\subtxt{i} \leq x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k} \leq x\subtxt{i+1}}^{i \geq j \geq k}\left(1-\frac{1}{2} \delta\subtxt{j,k}\right) \beta\subtxt{j,k} N\subtxt{j} N\subtxt{k}\left(\frac{x\subtxt{i+1}-x\subtxt{j} - x\subtxt{k}}{x\subtxt{i+1}-x\subtxt{i}}\right) \rho\subtxt{g} 
- N\subtxt{i} \sum_{k=1}^B \beta\subtxt{i,k} N\subtxt{k} \rho\subtxt{g}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
where \textit{B} is the total number of sections, $\delta\subtxt{j,k}$ is the Dirac delta function ($\delta\subtxt{j,k}=1$ if $j = k$, otherwise $\delta\subtxt{j,k}=0$), and $x\subtxt{i}$ is the volume of the pivot in the $i^{\text{th}}$ section with boundaries $v_i$ and $v\subtxt{i+1}$ (Figure~\ref{fig:Section}). $\beta\subtxt{j,k}$ is the Brownian collision frequency function between particles in the pivots $j^{\text{th}}$ and $k^{\text{th}}$, valid across all regimes, from the ballistic (free molecular) to the continuum (diffusive) regimes, and can be expressed as \cite{fuchs1965mechanics}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:beta_sectional}
\begin{gathered}
\beta\subtxt{j,k} = \beta\subtxt{co} \cdot \left[\frac{d\subtxt{c,j}+d\subtxt{c,k}}{d\subtxt{c,j}+d\subtxt{c,k}+2\bar\delta\subtxt{r}}+\frac{8\left(D\subtxt{j}+D\subtxt{k}\right)}{\bar{c}_r\left(d\subtxt{c,j}+d\subtxt{c,k}\right)}\right]^{-1}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
where $\beta\subtxt{co}$ is the collision frequency in the continuum regime with:


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:beta_sectional_CO}
\beta\subtxt{co} = 4\pi \left(\frac{d\subtxt{c,j}}{2} + \frac{d\subtxt{c,k}}{2}\right)\left(D\subtxt{j} + D\subtxt{k}\right)
\end{equation}

$\bar\delta\subtxt{r}$, $\bar{c}\subtxt{r}$, and $D$ are the transition correction factor, Brownian average velocity of particle, and diffusion coefficient, respectively.

The evolution of the particle volume concentration in the section $i$,  $V\subtxt{i}$, is defined as \cite{Tsantilis2002PopulationNanoparticles}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dVdt_sectional}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{d V\subtxt{i}}{d t}\bigg|\subtxt{agg}= \sum_{x\subtxt{i-1} \leq x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k} \leq x\subtxt{i+1}}^{i \geq j \geq k}\left(1-\frac{1}{2} \delta\subtxt{j, k}\right) \beta\subtxt{j, k}\left(N\subtxt{j} V\subtxt{k}+N\subtxt{k} V\subtxt{j}\right) \eta^{\prime} \rho\subtxt{g} 
- V\subtxt{i} \sum_{k=1}^B \beta\subtxt{i, k} N\subtxt{k} \rho\subtxt{g}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
where $\eta^{\prime}$ is the modified volume (interpolation) fraction:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:eta_sectional}
\begin{gathered}
\eta^{\prime} = 
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{\frac{x\subtxt{i+1}}{x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k}}-1}{\frac{x\subtxt{i+1}}{x\subtxt{i}}-1}, & x\subtxt{i} \leq x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k} \leq x\subtxt{i+1} \\[6pt]
\dfrac{\frac{x\subtxt{i-1}}{x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k}}-1}{\frac{x\subtxt{i-1}}{x\subtxt{i}}-1}, & x\subtxt{i-1} \leq x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k} \leq x\subtxt{i}
\end{cases}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
The rate of change of agglomerate area in the section $i$, $A_i$, is calculated as \cite{Tsantilis2002PopulationNanoparticles}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dAdt_sectional}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{d A\subtxt{i}}{d t}\bigg|\subtxt{agg}= \sum_{x\subtxt{i-1} \leq x\subtxt{j}+x\subtxt{k} \leq x\subtxt{i+1}}^{i \geq j \geq k}\left(1-\frac{1}{2} \delta\subtxt{j, k}\right) \beta\subtxt{j, k}\left(N\subtxt{j} A\subtxt{k}+N\subtxt{k} A\subtxt{j}\right) \eta^{\prime} \rho\subtxt{g} 
- A\subtxt{i} \sum_{k=1}^B \beta\subtxt{i, k} N\subtxt{k} \rho\subtxt{g}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}


\subsection{Sintering}
During the synthesis of NPs in an FCCVD reactor, fusion occurs to minimize surface free energy associated with curvature-induced excess chemical potential \cite{rahbar2023sintering} while conserving the total volume concentration ($V\subtxt{i}$).\ The rate of change of $A\subtxt{i}$ due to sintering is described as \cite{Tsantilis2001}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dAdt_sectional_sintering}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{dA\subtxt{i}}{dt}\bigg|\subtxt{sin} = -\frac{1}{\tau\subtxt{s,i}}\left(A\subtxt{i} - N\subtxt{i}\alpha\subtxt{i,s}\right)
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\text{s,i}}$ is the characteristic sintering time and $\alpha\subtxt{i,s} = a\subtxt{o}\left(V\subtxt{i}/N\subtxt{i}v\subtxt{0}\right)^{2/3}$ is the surface area of a fully sintered (coalesced) agglomerate in the $i^{\text{th}}$ section with $a_o$ and $v_o$ denoting the surface area and volume of a monomer, respectively. The parameter $\tau\subtxt{s}$ is defined as the time required for a 63\% reduction in the excess dimer surface area of two fusing PPs over that of a sphere of equal mass \cite{koch1990effect}. The $\tau\subtxt{s}$ for Ni \cite{rahbar2023sintering} and Fe \cite{Rosenberger2022} NPs is given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:taus_Ni_MD_derived_this_study}
\tau_{\mathrm{s,Ni}} =
\begin{cases}
6.9 \times 10^{15}\cdot T\subtxt{p}  d\subtxt{p}^4 
\exp \left(\dfrac{64\ 009}{R T\subtxt{p}}
\left(2.558 - \left(\dfrac{3.5 \times 10^{-9}}{d\subtxt{p}} \times \dfrac{T\subtxt{p}}{2100}\right)^3\right)\right), 
& T\subtxt{p} < T\subtxt{melt} \\[10pt]
\dfrac{\mu d\subtxt{p}}{\gamma}, 
& T\subtxt{p} \geq T\subtxt{melt}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}



\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sintering_fe_rosenberger}
\tau\subtxt{s,{Fe}} = A\subtxt{s,{Fe}} d\subtxt{p}^{\text{m}} \exp\left(\frac{E\subtxt{a,{Fe}}}{R T\subtxt{p}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $d\subtxt{p} = {6V\subtxt{i}}/  {A\subtxt{i}}$ is the PP diameter, $\mu$ \cite{miller1952physical} and $\gamma$ \cite{haynes2016crc} are the dynamic viscosity and surface tension of Ni, respectively, and $T\subtxt{melt} = -1.18 \times 10^3 \cdot d\subtxt{p}^{-0.9} + 1836$ $\mathrm{K}$ is the size-dependent melting temperature of Ni NPs \cite{rahbar2023sintering}.\  $A\subtxt{s,{Fe}}$ and $E\subtxt{a,{{Fe}}}$ are the pre-exponential factor and activation energy, respectively.\ The exponent $m$ describes the dominant mass transfer mechanism (i.e., grain-boundary diffusion, surface diffusion, and viscous flow) during the sintering process \cite{lunden1995sintering}. The particle temperature, $T\subtxt{p}$, is distinguished from that of the carrier gas temperature, $T$, by introducing \cite{lehtinen2002energy}:


\begin{equation}
\begin{gathered}
\label{eq:particle_temperature_sintering}
c\subtxt{v} n\subtxt{p} \frac{d T\subtxt{p}}{d t} = \frac{\gamma}{\tau\subtxt{s}}\left(\alpha - \alpha\subtxt{s}\right) - Z c\subtxt{g}\left(T\subtxt{p} - T\right) - \varepsilon\subtxt{p} \sigma\subtxt{SB} \alpha\subtxt{s}\left(T\subtxt{p}^4 - T^4\right)
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}
\\[6pt]
where $c\subtxt{v}$ and $c\subtxt{g}$ are the specific heats for the particle and carrier gas, respectively, $\varepsilon\subtxt{p}$ (${\sim}0.5$) is the emissivity of the particle\ \cite{yao2024spectral}, $\sigma_{\mathrm{SB}}$ is the Stefan-Boltzmann constant, and $Z = \alpha\subtxt{s} \mathrm{P}/(2\pi  m\subtxt{g}  k\subtxt{B} \mathrm{\textit{T}})^{0.5}$ is the collision rate between particles and carrier gas molecules with $P$ representing the reactor pressure and $m\subtxt{g}$ the carrier gas molecular mass. The first term on the right-hand side (RHS) in Eq.~\ref{eq:particle_temperature_sintering} 
represents an increase in the particle temperature due to the energy released during 
coalescence, during which the particle SSA decreases. The second and third RHS terms 
describe particle cooling by convective/conductive heat transfer and radiative 
losses, respectively.







\subsection{Inception and surface growth}


 
The rates of change of $N$, $V$, and $A$ due to the inception (i.e., formation of metal atoms from precursor decomposition) of PPs and their surface growth (i.e., heterogeneous atom deposition on PP surfaces) are defined as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dNdt_sectional_nucleation}
\frac{dN\subtxt{i}}{dt}\bigg|\subtxt{incep} = k\subtxt{g} C n\subtxt{i} \quad \text{with} \quad n\subtxt{i} = 
\begin{cases}
1 & v\subtxt{m} \in [v\subtxt{i}, v\subtxt{i+1}] \\
0 &v\subtxt{m} \notin [v\subtxt{i}, v\subtxt{i+1}]
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dAdt_sectional_nucleation}
\frac{dA\subtxt{i}}{dt}\bigg|\subtxt{incep+surf}  = k\subtxt{g} C \eta\subtxt{i} \alpha\subtxt{m} + 4\pi N\subtxt{i} n\subtxt{p,i} k\subtxt{s} d\subtxt{p,i} C v\subtxt{m} \rho\subtxt{g}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dxdt_sectional}
\frac{d x\subtxt{i}}{d t}|\subtxt{incep+surf}
=
k\subtxt{s} \, C \, n\subtxt{p,i} \, \pi d\subtxt{p,i}^{2} \, v\subtxt{m} \, \rho\subtxt{g}
\;+\;
\frac{1}{N\subtxt{i}}
\left( v\subtxt{m} - x\subtxt{i} \right)
\left( k\subtxt{g} C \right)
\eta\subtxt{i}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:dVdt_sectional_nucleation}
\frac{dV\subtxt{i}}{dt}\bigg|\subtxt{incep+surf}= \frac{d N\subtxt{i} x\subtxt{i} }{d t}\bigg|\subtxt{incep+surf}
\end{equation}
where $C$ is the precursor concentration with its homogeneous and heterogeneous decomposition rate constants of $k\subtxt{g}$ and $k\subtxt{s}$, respectively. Additionally, $v\subtxt{m}$ is the volume and $\alpha\subtxt{m}$ is the area of a single metallic atom.

\subsection{Multilayered model}
\label{subsec: Multilayered_Model}
This model was originally proposed by Puretzky et al. \cite{Puretzky2005InDeposition} to describe kinetic data measured in a substrate-based CVD reactor using acetylene (C\(_2\)H\(_2\)) as the carbon feedstock and iron-molybdenum NPs as the catalysts. The model accounts for activation energies and rate constants associated with surface carbon formation and diffusion into the NPs.\ The number of carbon atoms incorporated into a CNT ($N\subtxt{t}$) is 
determined by tracking several carbon-related populations on the surface of the NPs:\ (i) carbon atoms on the surface of the NPs ($N\subtxt{C}$), formed due to catalytic decomposition of C\(_2\)H\(_2\);\ (ii) carbon atoms converted into the carbonaceous (platelet) layer on the NP surface ($N\subtxt{L1}$),\ (iii) inactive catalyst atoms on the NP surface ($N\subtxt{L2}$), and (iv) carbon atoms diffusing into the bulk region of NPs ($N\subtxt{B}$). The schematic in Figure~\ref{fig:Schematic} illustrates the model, accounting for the adsorption of C\(_2\)H\(_2\), carbon surface and bulk diffusion, formation of carbon platelets, and hydrogasification process (C$_{\text{s}}$ + 2H$_2$$\ \rightarrow$ CH$_4$), along with the inception of graphene layers and CNT on the surface of NPs.\ The C\(_2\)H\(_2\) flux, $F\subtxt{b1}$, and its main pyrolysis products (e.g., C\(_4\)H\(_4\) \cite{Ma2011ModellingDeposition}), $F\subtxt{b2}$, toward the surface of NPs within section $i$ are given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_fb1}
F\subtxt{b1} = \frac{1}{4} A\subtxt{p} q \left( \frac{k\subtxt{B} T}{2\pi m} \right)^{1/2}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_fb2}
F\subtxt{b2} = \frac{1}{4} A\subtxt{p} q\subtxt{p} \left( \frac{k\subtxt{B} T}{2\pi M} \right)^{1/2}
\end{equation}
where $A\subtxt{p} = A\subtxt{i}/N\subtxt{i}$ is the surface area of one agglomerate in section $i$, $q$, $q\subtxt{p}$ are the number concentrations of the C$_2$H$_2$ and its main pyrolysis product (e.g., C\(_4\)H\(_4\)) in the carrier gas mixture, and $m$ and $M$ are the masses of C$_2$H$_2$ molecule and its major pyrolysis product, respectively.\ The corresponding carbon fluxes due to $F\subtxt{b1}$ and $F\subtxt{b2}$ are:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_fc1}
F\subtxt{c1} = F\subtxt{b1} p\subtxt{1} \exp \left(-\frac{E\subtxt{a1}}{R T}\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_fc2}
F\subtxt{c2} = F\subtxt{b2} p\subtxt{2} \exp \left(-\frac{E\subtxt{a2}}{R T}\right)
\end{equation}
where $p\subtxt{1}=p\subtxt{2}=1$ are the pre-exponential factors and $E\subtxt{a1} = E\subtxt{a2}=57\ 891\, \ \mathrm{J/mol}$ are the activation energies for catalytic decomposition of the C$_2$H$_2$ and its major products, respectively \cite{Puretzky2005InDeposition}. The processes discussed above can be described by the following set of differential equations as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_dNcdt}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{d N\subtxt{C}}{d t} = {F}\subtxt{c1}\left(1- \frac{N\subtxt{L1}+N\subtxt{L2}}{\alpha A\subtxt{p} n\subtxt{m}}\right) - (k\subtxt{sb}+k\subtxt{cl}+k\subtxt{e}) N\subtxt{C}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_dNL1dt}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{d N\subtxt{L1}}{d t} = {F}\subtxt{c2}\left(1-\frac{N\subtxt{L1}+N\subtxt{L2}}{\alpha A\subtxt{p} n\subtxt{m}}\right) + k\subtxt{cl} N\subtxt{C} - (k\subtxt{d1}+k\subtxt{e}) N\subtxt{L1}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_dNL2dt}
\begin{gathered}
\frac{d N\subtxt{L2}}{d t} = k\subtxt{r}\left(1-\frac{N\subtxt{L1}+N\subtxt{L2}}{\alpha A\subtxt{p} n\subtxt{m}}\right) - k\subtxt{d2} N\subtxt{L2}
\end{gathered}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_dNBdt}
\frac{d N\subtxt{B}}{d t} = k\subtxt{s b} N\subtxt{C} - k\subtxt{t} N\subtxt{B} + k\subtxt{d1} N\subtxt{L1}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_dNtdt}
\frac{d N\subtxt{t}}{d t} = k\subtxt{t} N\subtxt{B}
\end{equation}
where $n\subtxt{m}=10^{19}~\mathrm{\#/m^2}$ and $\alpha$~\cite{Puretzky2005InDeposition} are the surface density and number of carbon monolayers, respectively, $k\subtxt{sb}$ the rate constant for surface–to-bulk penetration of carbon atoms, $k\subtxt{cl}$ the rate for platelet formation, $k\subtxt{e}$ the rate of carbon atom removal due to methane (CH$_4$) formation through the etching process \cite{Gil2023InvestigationHydrogasification}, and $k\subtxt{t}$ is the rate for carbon diffusion to the CNT/graphene growth sites region with \cite{Puretzky2005InDeposition}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_ksb}
k\subtxt{sb} = 2.72 \times 10^{14} \exp\left(-\frac{212\ 267}{R T}\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_kcl}
k\subtxt{cl} \approx 3 \times 10^{-3}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_ke}
k\subtxt{e} = \frac{ (1.15 \times 10^{-13}) \exp\left(-\frac{72\ 100}{R T}\right) P_{\mathrm{H}_2}^2 }{ 1 + (3.50 \times 10^{-12}) \exp\left(-\frac{118\ 100}{R T}\right) P_{\mathrm{H}_2} }
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_kt}
k\subtxt{t} = \left(\frac{2 \times 10^{-4}}{d\subtxt{p}^2}\right) \exp\left(-\frac{144\ 728}{R T}\right)
\end{equation}

Here, $k\subtxt{d1}$ and $k\subtxt{d2}$ are the rate constants corresponding to the dissolution of the platelets and the reactivation of the deactivated layer (caused by the presence of water), respectively, and both are assumed to be negligible (i.e., $k\subtxt{d1} = k\subtxt{d2}=0$) \cite{Puretzky2005InDeposition}.
The rate of catalyst deactivation due to H$_2$ diffusion, $k\subtxt{r}$, can also be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_kr}
k\subtxt{r} = 1.2 \times 10^{8} F_{\mathrm{H}_2} \exp\left(-\frac{231\ 564}{R T}\right)
\end{equation}
where the H$_2$ flux into the NP ($F_{\mathrm{H}_2}$) can be estimated from its partial pressure ($P_{\mathrm{H}_2}$) as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_FH2}
F_{\mathrm{H}_2} =2 \alpha_{\mathrm{H}_2} D_{\mathrm{H}_2} \frac{N\subtxt{Avo} P_{\mathrm{H}_2}}{RT} \frac{A}{d\subtxt{p}}
\end{equation}
with a sticking probability of \(\alpha_{\mathrm{H}_2} = 7\times 10^{-4}\) and an H\(_2\) diffusion coefficient of \(D_{\mathrm{H}_2}= 5 \times 10^{-8} \exp\left(-\frac{10\ 958}{RT}\right)\).  The number of walls, $N_\mathrm{w}$, can be determined from the thickness of the disordered layer (Figure~\ref{fig:Schematic}a) expressed as the number of carbon monolayers:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_Nw}
N\subtxt{w} = 
\begin{cases}
N\subtxt{C}/{\tilde{\alpha} A\subtxt{p} n\subtxt{m}} & \text{if } N\subtxt{w} > 1 \\[8pt]
1 & \text{if } N\subtxt{w} \leq 1
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{\alpha}$ is an adjustable parameter tuned to the experimental data, with $\tilde{\alpha}=0.3$ \cite{Puretzky2005InDeposition}.

\subsection{Dual-dissociation model}
\label{subsec: Dual-dissociation model}

This model was developed by Ma \textit{et al.}~\cite{Ma2011ModellingDeposition} to predict the growth rate of MWCNTs in a substrate-based CVD reactor from the decomposition of C$_2$H$_2$ and C$_4$H$_4$ (the major decomposition product).\ Three key steps govern the synthesis process under continuous feedstock supply:\ (i) adsorption and dissociation of C$_2$H$_2$ and C$_4$H$_4$ on the Fe NP surface, i.e., C$_2$H$_2$ $\rightarrow$ C$_{\text{2,s}}$ + H$_2$ and C$_4$H$_4$ $\rightarrow$ C$_{\text{4,s}}$ + 2H$_2$, where the subscript "s" denotes species on the NP surface;\ (ii) carbon–metal solid solution or carbide formation; and\ (iii) carbon precipitation from supersaturated NPs (i.e., C$_{\text{2,s}}$$\rightarrow$ 2C$\subtxt{cnt}$ and C$_{\text{4,s}}$   $\rightarrow$ 4C$\subtxt{cnt}$). Therefore, the number of carbon atoms precipitated into the CNT ($N\subtxt{t}$) is obtained by tracking the number of carbon atoms dissolved in the NP ($N\subtxt{B}$) as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:Ma_Nt}
\frac{dN\subtxt{t}}{dt} =\frac{dN\subtxt{B}}{dt}
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_Ma_dNBdt}
\frac{dN\subtxt{B}}{dt} = A\subtxt{p}\gamma\subtxt{p}\rho\subtxt{gas}\sqrt{\frac{k_{B}T}{2\pi}} \frac{[O_{\text{s}}]}{{[O_{\text{s,o}}]}}(\frac{[C_{\text{C}_2\text{H}_2}]}{\sqrt{m}}+\frac{[C_{\text{C}_4\text{H}_4}]}{\sqrt{M}})
\end{equation}
where $\gamma\subtxt{p}$ is the sticking probability of reactant species adsorbing onto the surface of NPs, [C$\subtxt{n}$H$\subtxt{m}$], [O$\subtxt{s,o}$], and [O$\subtxt{s}$] are the concentrations of C$\subtxt{n}$H$\subtxt{m}$, as well as the initial and current active site concentrations on the NP surface, respectively:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_Ma_gammap}
\gamma\subtxt{p} =
\begin{cases}
6.38 \times 10^{-3} \exp(1.11 \times 10^{-2} (T - 823.81)), &  T \leq 973 \\
3.46 \times 10^{-2} \exp(-3.27 \times 10^{-2} (T - 973)), &  T > 973
\end{cases}
\end{equation}


Although one of the advantages of the dual-dissociation model is its ability to track the concentration of adsorption sites, it does not predict the number of walls due to the lack of information about the thickness of the disordered layer.\ Instead, as an explicit assumption, the model assumes the inner diameter of the CNT is half of its outer diameter, i.e., $d\subtxt{cnt,inner}=0.5\  d\subtxt{cnt,outer}$.\ In this package, the outer diameter of the CNT produced in an FCCVD reactor is calculated from the PP diameter of the NPs, as statistical measurements by Nasibulin et al. \cite{nasibulin2005correlation} showed that the ratio between the catalyst and the CNT diameters remains close to 1.6 (i.e., $d\subtxt{cnt} \approx d\subtxt{p}/1.6$), regardless of process conditions.

\subsection{User\text{-}defined kinetics (UDK)}
\label{sec:sec_UDF}
To further enhance the flexibility of the package, a user-defined kinetics (UDK) submodule is included, enabling the integration of custom surface kinetics models.\ The user-defined reaction pathways can be specified in a YAML input file, following the standard format used for surface mechanisms, including site adsorption/desorption, hydrogen abstraction, and carbon diffusion (e.g., Table 1 in \cite{lysaght2008modeling}). The submodule enables full coupling between user-specified surface reactions and the background gas chemistry solved by the chemical kinetics submodule. 


\section{CNT dynamics}
\label{sec:CNT_dynamics}


Before the onset of CNT in the reactor, carbon is assumed to dissolve into the nanoparticles until it reaches saturation, beyond which no additional carbon can dissolve \cite{nasibulin2005correlation}.\ The expressions for size-dependent carbon solubility of Ni and Fe NPs were proposed by Moisala et al. \cite{Moisala2003} as:


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_kraft_saturation_Ni}
\frac{\mathrm{N}_{\text {sat }}}{\mathrm{N}_{\text {Ni }}} = \left(0.01775+5.219 \times 10^{-5} {~T}\right) \exp \left(\frac{4 \nu_{\mathrm{Ni}}\gamma\subtxt{Ni} }{\mathrm{k}_{\mathrm{B}} {T}\mathrm{d}_{\mathrm{p}}}\right)
\end{equation}


\begin{equation}
\label{eq:surf_kin_kraft_saturation_Fe}
\frac{\mathrm{N}_{\text {sat }}}{\mathrm{N}_{\text {Fe }}} = \left(0.062305+1.176 \times 10^{-4} {~T}\right) \exp \left(\frac{4\nu_{\mathrm{Fe}} \gamma\subtxt{Fe} }{\mathrm{k}_{\mathrm{B}} T\mathrm{d}_{\mathrm{p}}}\right)
\end{equation}
where ${N}\subtxt{Ni}$ and ${N}\subtxt{Fe}$ are the number of metal atoms (monomers) in a PP, $\gamma\subtxt{Ni} = 2$ \cite{haynes2016crc} and $\gamma\subtxt{Fe} = 1.72$ \cite{Moisala2003} are surface tensions, and $\nu\subtxt{Ni}$ and $\nu\subtxt{Fe}$ are the atomic volumes for Ni and Fe, respectively.\ 



From Eqs.~\ref{eq:surf_kin_puretzki_dNtdt} and~\ref{eq:Ma_Nt}, under carbon-saturated conditions,  the rates of carbon deposition into the CNT and graphene layers can be defined as \cite{Celnik2008}:
\begin{align}
\frac{dL\subtxt{cnt}}{dt} &=f\subtxt{cnt} \left(\frac{m\subtxt{c}}{\pi\rho\subtxt{c}\delta\subtxt{gp}d\subtxt{cnt}}\right)\frac{dN\subtxt{t}}{dt}\\
\frac{dA\subtxt{gp}}{dt} &=(1-f\subtxt{cnt}) \left(\frac{m\subtxt{c}}{\pi\rho\subtxt{c}\delta\subtxt{gp}}\right)\frac{dN\subtxt{t}}{dt}
\end{align}
where $f\subtxt{cnt}$ is the fraction of carbon atoms ($N\subtxt{t}$) that contribute to the formation of CNTs, $m_c$ is the mass of one carbon atom, $\rho\subtxt{c}=1.8 ~$$\mathrm{g/cm^3}$ and $\delta\subtxt{gp}=0.34~$$\mathrm{nm}$ are the graphite density and graphene layer thickness, respectively \cite{patterson2006extending}.\ The mobility diameter of agglomerates can be correlated with the average number of PPs per agglomerate, valid for all Knudsen regimes:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PBM_dm}
d\subtxt{m}^{\text{NP}} = d\subtxt{p} n\subtxt{p}^{0.45}
\end{equation}
where the average number of PPs in each agglomerate, \( n\subtxt{p} \), is \cite{kelesidis2017morphology}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PBM_np}
n\subtxt{p} = \frac{6V}{N \pi d\subtxt{p}^3}
\end{equation}

Also, the gyration diameter, $d\subtxt{g}^{\text{NP}}$, is calculated as \cite{kelesidis2017morphology}:
\begin{equation}
d\subtxt{g}^{\text{NP}} = \begin{cases}
d\subtxt{m}^{\text{NP}} \left(n\subtxt{p}^{-0.2} + 0.4\right)^{-1}, & n\subtxt{p} > 1.8 \\
d\subtxt{m}^{\text{NP}} \sqrt{\frac{3}{5}}, & n\subtxt{p} \leq 1.8
\end{cases}
\end{equation}


 From TDMA measurements, Kim and Zachariah\ \cite{Kim2006In-flightClassification} proposed an expression for the on-the-fly mobility diameter (units in nm) of CNT as:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:PBM_dm_cnt}
d\subtxt{m}^{\text{cnt}}  = 8.61\times 10^{-1}\sqrt{{\frac{4 d\subtxt{cnt} \mathrm{L}\subtxt{cnt}}{\pi} }}+ 7.6
\end{equation} 

\section{Iteration scheme }
Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart_dateiled} illustrates the workflow of the CarbonX package, which consists of three main components: input (steps 1-3), reactor modeling (steps 4-10), and output handling (step 11).\ The key components of the user input include chemical kinetics (FFCM-2, Caltech, ABF, etc.), surface kinetics (multilayered, dual-dissociation, and user-defined), and particle dynamics model name specifications, as well as general configuration settings (e.g., total number of simulations along with residence time, temperature-time history, flow rates, reactor pressure, etc.). In steps 4-5, the gas chemistry of the system is solved using the Cantera-UDK submodule, where thermodynamic (e.g., density and pressure) and transport (e.g., viscosity) properties, and species mole fractions are obtained incrementally. 

Current NP population and morphology data are passed to the surface kinetics submodule to calculate carbon fluxes in the bulk, multilayered, and CNT site regions.\ The states of NPs in terms of encapsulation and poisoning are also evaluated.\ In step 7, the NP morphology is updated within each bin, followed by steps 8-9, where the CNT and graphene characteristics (i.e.,  $d_{\mathrm{cnt}}$, $L_{\mathrm{cnt}}$,  and $A_{\mathrm{gp}}$) are estimated. 

Once a simulation cycle is completed (step 10), the process is repeated until all points in the 2D characteristic maps are generated across the selected (range of) process conditions (e.g., $P_{\mathrm{\text{C}_2\text{H}_2}}$ vs. $T$, $N$ vs.\ $T$, and $d_{\mathrm{\text{p}}}$ vs.\ $T$). Finally, the machine-learning-based classification submodule is activated (step 11) to classify the predicted maps into regions corresponding to the process conditions that yield SWCNTs and MWCNTs in FCCVD reactors.
